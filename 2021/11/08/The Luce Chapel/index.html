The Luce Chapel
Date: 17/07/2016, Photographer: Miss S, Post-Processing: Miss Shuya

This photograph was taken at Tunghai University, a Christian academy located in the Dadu Mountain. In 1955, students there used to live a self-sufficient lifestyle due to inaccessibility. The United States Board of Directors was deeply aware of the lack of spiritual presence around campus, and hence they decided to build a worship venue, The Luce Chapel here [1]. The chapel was designed by architects I. M. Pei [2] and Chi-Kuan Chen presumably taking inspiration from Yale University Art Gallery, and it was named in honor of the Rev. Henry W. Luce, an American missionary in China [3].

The design of the building is unique: a four-walled symmetrical structure, an irregular hexagonal base, and a cross on the top. I am fascinated by the perfect symmetry of architecture. It has been scientifically proven that architecture can affect our mood and well-being [4]. To be more specific, specialized cells in the hippocampus of our brains are attuned to the geometry and arrangement of the spaces we inhabit [5, 6]. That explains the intrinsic feeling of satisfaction and elation I get from observing symmetrical buildings. The picture below shows the architectural structure of the church [1].

Compared to the modern construction, I prefer historical counterparts, as they are the epitomes of life in the past. Additionally, antique buildings help enhance my understanding of ancient history.

I would like to conclude my thoughts with logion wisdom by a famous American architect Frank Lloyd Wright: “The mother art is architecture. Without an architecture of our own, we have no soul of our own civilization.”
